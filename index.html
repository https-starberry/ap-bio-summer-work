What are 3 abiotic and 3 biotic factors of the ocean and how do they sustain the ecosystem?
jelly_jellyfish
I can answer this question! Three abiotic factors are temperature, salinity, and solar energy. Solar energy serves as the basis for all life, so of course it's the same for life underwater. Aquatic plants use sunlight for photosynthesis which fuels the food web. Salinity is another one as many species of fish in the ocean are accustomed to the saltiness of the ocean. If it becomes too salty or not salty enough, these organisms would struggle to adapt as they're not accustomed to the salinity fluctuating. Lastly, temperature affects the survival, development, and reproduction of many oceanic species. When the temperature is just right, these creatures will behave as expected and observed. But if it changes then they will attempt to migrate for food, reproduction, and survival in general.
orca_fangirl
This is part of the reason why climate change is such a large issue today. Not only will it cause the polar ice caps to melt, marine heat waves, and acidification of the ocean, it will also negatively affect the ocean's biodiversity. Many species will migrate to find cooler waters which may interrupt ecosystems that aren't capable of handling such a large influx. Right now, there's been a 1.1℃ in temperature which means about 60% of these ecosystems have been degraded. If it increases to 1.5℃, then it will jump to anywhere between 70% and 90%. Should it ever reach a 2℃ increase in temperature then we've officially reached the point of no return.
cute_sharks
Does this tie into biotic factors as well?
jelly_jellyfish
It does, unfortunately. Sharks, for example, help maintain the populations of fish they eat. Without sharks, these fish would begin to eat away at the populations of producers (like algae and phytoplankton) which would have disastrous consequences on other species that also consume these producers for survival. Phytoplankton helps to fuel the food web. Without them, fish wouldn't have a food source and therefore would affect the levels above them. Lastly, coral reefs are depended on by fish to provide shelter to raise their young and reproduce. They also allow fish to find food. Without them, these fish would struggle to survive and reproduce. Biotic factors like these will suffer under climate change and if invasive species are added to the mix, it'll worsen the situation exponentially.
orca_fangirl
Invasive species?
cute_sharks
Invasive species are species that are taken from one area and introduced into another area. They're non-native to the new habitat they've been introduced to.
orca_fangirl
What would happen if they're introduced, though?
cute_sharks
When they're introduced to a new habitat, they don't have any natural predators so they're able to reproduce freely. Because of this, they usually overpower native species and outcompete them for resources. They can also disrupt the habitat by invading and claiming territories for themselves. There's also the chance they could change the quality of the water, spread diseases, and increase competition.
orca_fangirl
So would it be an example of parasitism?
cute_sharks
No, since parasitism involves a host and a "parasite." The "parasite" usually lives in or with the host and slowly kills them off which allows them to reap the benefits. A benefit could be having an organism to lay its eggs in. An example of one would be swimming crabs and barnacles.
jelly_jellyfish
That pairing is interesting because barnacles don't cause any harm to whales. The relationship between these two is called commensalism. This relationship only requires that one party benefits and the other doesn't benefit or lose from engaging in the relationship. Whales don't gain anything from this relationship, but the barnacles do.
orca_fangirl
So it's a bit different from mutualism where both parties benefit from their relationship like clownfish and anemones.
cute_sharks
But these are different from predator and prey relationships where the predator hunts the prey for nutrition. For example, tiger sharks are the predator and a keystone specie and the green sea turtle is the prey.
orca_fangirl
Yeah, like sea slugs are the predators and sea anemones are the prey or small scale-scorpionfish are the predators and the goby is the prey.

I can assume we all know competition is important in maintaining balance in ecosystems. What are some examples of competition in the ocean?
orca_fangirl
An example of competition for food would be sponges and coral. If sponges are too successful they end up monopolizing food that the coral also needs to survive. But this tipping on the scale towards sponges will never last too long because once coral start to die off the reef is damaged and the sponges follow soon after. A balance in competition has to be maintained for the ecosystem to prosper.
orca_fangirl
Competition for finding a mate is another example. Dall's porpoises have a ritual called "mate guarding." Once a male finds a partner he guards her to make sure she isn't able to mate with other males. So males without partners are constantly fighting with males with partners for a chance to reproduce.
jelly_jellyfish
There's also competition for territory. Peacock mantis shrimp are SUPER territorial. They only ever interact with each other during mating season, but then return to solitude once it's over. They use stomatopod rumbles or vibrations to communicate warnings to other mantis shrimp and predators. If a mantis shrimp gets too close to its territory it will use its colorful body to ward them off.
orca_fangirl
In general this just shows that the ocean has a wide array of adaptations.
jelly_jellyfish
Like what?
orca_fangirl
Like kelp, seaweed, angler fish, and burrowing crabs. They each have unique adaptations to deal with living underwater.
orca_fangirl
Kelp has gas-filled floats (pneumatocysts) to help buoy their leaves close to the sun. This allows them to perform photosynthesis easier. Seaweed on the other hand can take in nutrients from anywhere in the body (thallus), not just their roots like "normal" plants do.
orca_fangirl
Angler fish adapted to have a gary or dark brown color to their skin to avoid predators and to blend in with their surroundings. Burrowing crabs burrow to not only protect themselves but to create a safe place to lay their eggs.
cute_sharks
But what about innate and learned behaviors?
jelly_jellyfish
Sea turtles are great examples of innate behaviors. Once sea turtles have hatched they immediately know they have to reach the ocean as fast as possible as there are predators that actively wait for them to hatch. Baby sea turtles instinctively move in the direction that has the most amount of light. If you think about it, it makes sense as to why. Since they hatch at night, the brightest light source (usually) is the moon. Lastly, sea turtles can't breathe underwater. They don't have gills, so they have to resurface to get the oxygen they need. As sea turtles don't raise their young they're born with this instinct of resurfacing.
orca_fangirl
Orcas are prime examples of learned behaviors. Just like humans, they have a language, distinct culture, and cultural pride, and pass on information orally. Within the Orca language, there are dialects. These dialects are the core of each pod's culture. A transient Orca's dialect is starkly different from a resident Orca's dialect. Calves learn their pod's language by mimicking the other orcas within their pod. At first, these are clumsy attempts but eventually, they learn what context to use certain whistles, calls, and clicks.
orca_fangirl
Recently, members of a pod on the coast of Argentina have been observed purposefully beaching itself.
cute_sharks
Beaching themselves?
orca_fangirl
As disturbing as it sounds, it's a hunting tactic that can take years to learn. This method is often taught by elders of the pods to the calves. Some members of the pod never learn this technique as the feeling of their body out of water is enough to psych them out. The ones that do learn how to become exceptionally great hunters.
orca_fangirl
Just like any human culture, Orcas teach and pass their pod's culture to their children. So they not only teach hunting strategies and their language but also what foods they choose to eat, how to interact with other Orcas, what species are their friends and who are their foes, etc. We know that this is true because it's been observed countless times that calves learn through mimicry and not innate behaviors like sea turtles. It makes sense as to why they evolved to be this way. Their parents and other relatives raise and teach them how to survive.
